001 : Pore Network Models (PNMs)

This image shows a 3D, regular lattice of pores (i.e. spheres) connected with throats (i.e. black lines). Maximum and minimum pore connectivity (a.k.a. coordination number) are 6 and 3, respectively.

PNMs follow a very simple idea: breaking down the porous structure into its basic elements; i.e. 1) pores which are responsible to hold the main volume of different phases, and 2) throats which carry the fluids from one pore to the neiboring one. Developing PNM is twofold; first a network of ceonnected pores and throats should be constructed, and then, on top of this network, rules for evolution of different phases and solutes should be defined.

During my MSc, I was developing a quasi-static PNM for modelling three-phase flow. I started with a regular random network with max coordination number of 6, and later, extended it to 26 (which I borrowed the idea from Lattice Boltzmann's D3Q27). I started modifying the network properties by randomely removing connections, which would make the network more representative of that of a real porous structure (like Bentheimer Sandstone). I also tried to use not only spheres, but rather more angular shapes for both pores and throats, as they mimic the topology of a pore more accurately. For the last stage of fine-tuning the network, I designed a Genetic-Algorithm scheme to match experimental capillary pressure curve. I used the tuned model to predict on two-phase relative permeability curves, which have shown a surprisingly good match!
